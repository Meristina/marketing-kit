# Skill — Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)

Reframe the market around the **progress the customer is trying to make**, not the
product's features or the buyer's demographics. A "job" is the progress a person seeks
in a given circumstance. People *hire* a product to make that progress and *fire* it when
something makes better progress.

> **Doctrine:** JTBD is canonical but contested in its details. Cite Christensen et al.
> (HBR, Sept 2016) as the canonical reference; acknowledge origins (Ulwick's
> Outcome-Driven Innovation, 2002; Levitt's "people don't want a drill, they want a
> hole"). **Never invent a quote, statistic, or market size** — research every external
> fact on the internet and cite it.

## Procedure
1. **Frame the circumstance.** Who, in what situation, with what trigger? A job lives in
   a *context* ("when I ___, I want to ___, so I can ___"), not in a persona.
2. **Surface the struggling moment.** What pushed them to look for something new? The job
   is born from a struggle, an unmet desire for progress.
3. **Decompose the job into three dimensions:**
   - **Functional** — the practical task to accomplish.
   - **Emotional** — how they want to *feel* (or stop feeling).
   - **Social** — how they want to be *perceived* by others.
4. **Map the Forces of Progress** (the switching equation):
   - **Push** of the current situation (what's broken today).
   - **Pull** of the new solution (the magnetism of the promised progress).
   - **Anxiety** of the new (risk, learning cost, uncertainty).
   - **Habit** of the present (inertia, sunk cost, "good enough").
   Switching happens only when **Push + Pull > Anxiety + Habit**.
5. **Identify the real competitors** — everything the customer could "hire" for the same
   job, *including non-consumption and unrelated products* (the milkshake competes with
   bananas and boredom, not other milkshakes).
6. **Write the job statement(s)** in the canonical form and rank by frequency × intensity
   × current under-service. Flag which jobs are over-served vs under-served.
7. **Hand off:** the dominant jobs and their forces feed Category Entry Points (Officer 1),
   positioning & value-prop (Officer 2), and conversion friction analysis (Officer 4).
