# Skill — Porter's Five Forces

Judge how attractive an industry/segment is by the forces that erode profitability. Strong
forces = profit competed away; weak forces = structurally attractive. Analyse the **industry
the brand competes in** (define it precisely — too broad and the forces blur), at a point in
time, and note the direction of travel.

> **Doctrine — structure, not a competitor list.** Five Forces is about the *industry's
> structure*, not a tactical competitor scan. Define the industry boundary explicitly,
> rate each force **High / Medium / Low with evidence**, and translate to *where profit is*
> and *what to defend*. Optional 6th lens: **complementors** (ecosystem/platform plays).
> **Never invent** a share, margin, or switching-cost figure — source it or label
> `[assumption — verify]`; unknown → "unknown".

## The five forces (rate each H/M/L with evidence)
1. **Competitive rivalry** — number & balance of competitors, industry growth, fixed costs/
   exit barriers, differentiation, price competition. (High when many equal rivals, slow
   growth, low differentiation.)
2. **Threat of new entrants** — entry barriers: capital, economies of scale, brand/access to
   channels, switching costs, regulation, incumbents' likely retaliation. (High threat =
   low barriers.)
3. **Threat of substitutes** — alternatives *outside* the industry that meet the same need
   (incl. non-consumption / DIY); their price-performance trend and buyer's switching cost.
4. **Bargaining power of buyers** — concentration, volume, price sensitivity, switching
   costs, backward-integration threat, information symmetry. (High when few large buyers,
   undifferentiated product.)
5. **Bargaining power of suppliers** — concentration, uniqueness of input, switching costs,
   forward-integration threat. (High when few suppliers of a critical input.)

## Procedure
1. **Define the industry boundary** precisely (product/service, geography, segment, time).
   Ambiguity here invalidates the analysis. Note B2B vs B2C.
2. **Rate each force H/M/L**, each with the 1–3 structural drivers that justify the rating
   and a sourced data point where one exists. Note **direction** (rising/falling) and *why*.
3. **Overall attractiveness verdict**: synthesize the five into one read (attractive /
   mixed / unattractive) — it is not an average; name the dominant force(s) that decide it.
4. **Strategic implications**: where profit pools sit, which force to neutralise or avoid,
   how to build a defensible position (raise barriers, reduce buyer/supplier power, escape
   rivalry via differentiation/focus — ties back to STP & positioning).
5. **Hand off:** the structural read informs targeting/positioning (O2), where to build
   brand moats (O3), and which channels have power over you (O4). Flag gaps for the Inspector.
