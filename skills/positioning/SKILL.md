# Skill — Positioning

Make explicit the place the brand should own in the mind of the chosen target. Positioning
is **relative** (defined against the alternatives the customer would otherwise pick) and
lives in the customer's head, not the product spec.

> **Two lenses, combined:**
> - **Ries & Trout** — the battle is for a position in the mind; own a word/idea; be first
>   or redefine the frame; position *against* the leader. Simplicity and consistency win.
> - **April Dunford** — build positioning from evidence, in order: competitive alternatives
>   → unique attributes → the value those attributes enable → the customers who care most →
>   the market frame that makes the value obvious (and optionally a trend that adds urgency).
>
> **Doctrine — sharpen, don't re-decide.** STP already chose the target and set the anchor
> (target / frame / point of difference / reason to believe). You **craft and stress-test
> the statement**; you do not re-pick the segment. **Never invent** a proof point, a
> competitor claim, or a "leader in X" — source it or drop it; unknown → "unknown".

## Procedure
1. **Take the STP anchor** as input (target, frame of reference, point of difference, reason
   to believe). If absent, request/run STP first — don't invent a target.
2. **Map competitive alternatives (Dunford):** what the target would use if you didn't
   exist — including non-consumption and the status quo. Positioning is relative to these.
3. **Unique attributes → value:** list what you have that the alternatives don't, then
   translate each attribute into the **value** it delivers to the target (attributes are
   features; value is what the customer gets). Drop attributes that don't ladder to value.
4. **Best-fit customers:** which target characteristics make them care *most* about that
   value (ties back to the chosen segment + JTBD/CEPs).
5. **Market frame of reference:** the category you want to be evaluated in — the frame that
   makes your value obvious and your difference decisive. Test alternative frames; the frame
   is a choice with consequences.
6. **Write the positioning statement** (classic form), then a one-line **mind-claim**
   (the word/idea to own, Ries & Trout):
   - *For [target] who [need/JTBD], [brand] is the [frame of reference] that [point of
     difference], because [reason to believe].*
7. **Stress-test (mandatory):** is it **relevant** (target cares), **distinctive** (not a
   category-generic claim every rival could say), **credible** (proof exists), **durable**
   (defensible as competitors react), and **simple** (one idea, not five)? Flag failures.
8. **Hand off:** the statement + the word-to-own feed brand-building (O3 messaging,
   distinctive assets, CEP links) and demand (O4 copy). Note proof gaps for the Inspector.

## Output format
```
POSITIONING — <brand / product>
Context detected: <B2B/B2C · market · stage>
Anchor in (from STP): target · frame · point of difference · reason to believe

COMPETITIVE ALTERNATIVES (incl. status quo / non-consumption)
  - …

UNIQUE ATTRIBUTES → VALUE
  | Attribute (we have, they don't) | Value to the target |
  …

BEST-FIT CUSTOMERS — who cares most & why (ties to segment + JTBD/CEPs)

MARKET FRAME OF REFERENCE — chosen frame + why it beats the alternatives considered

POSITIONING STATEMENT
  "For <target> who <need/JTBD>, <brand> is the <frame> that <point of difference>,
   because <reason to believe>."
MIND-CLAIM (the word/idea to own): "<…>"

STRESS-TEST  | Relevant? | Distinctive? | Credible? | Durable? | Simple? |  + fixes

SOURCES (every proof point / competitor claim cited; nothing invented)
  - <competitor / proof sources researched live>  · STP (the anchor).
```
